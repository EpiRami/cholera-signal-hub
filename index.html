Context: Cholera in Yemen

Cholera (acute watery diarrhoea caused by Vibrio cholerae) spreads rapidly where water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) systems are disrupted. Yemen has seen recurrent cholera/AWD surges over multiple years, with risk amplified by displacement, water scarcity, and fragile health services.

MSF (Médecins Sans Frontières) typically intervenes via cholera treatment centres/units (CTCs/CTUs), oral rehydration points (ORPs), community outreach, and WASH support (chlorination, waste management), alongside IPC training and support to health staff. This prototype collates public signals to assist rapid situational awareness and decision support. It does not replace surveillance or official reporting.

Interpretation

Suspected cholera and acute watery diarrhoea (AWD) cases have been consistently reported in Amran Governorate over the past year. Although this demo dataset represents a limited subset, the pattern mirrors field observations across northern Yemen, where population movements, reduced water quality, and fragile health infrastructure continue to sustain transmission risks.

The synthetic linelist indicates that most suspected cases occurred among children under 15 years and females were slightly predominant, reflecting typical vulnerability profiles in household-based exposures. The majority of patients presented with moderate to severe dehydration, suggesting delays in seeking care or limited access to oral rehydration points. Treatment outcomes were favorable overall, with high recovery rates and few recorded deaths, highlighting the importance of early fluid replacement and accessible cholera treatment units (CTUs).

Spatially, all records originate from Amran, underscoring the need for subdistrict-level mapping and case confirmation to differentiate localized contamination from broader community spread. Temporal clustering around the mid-year rainy season (June–August) corresponds to known seasonal peaks driven by flooding and water stagnation.

Complementary public reports from MSF and the UNICEF/WASH Cluster between October 2024 and September 2025 describe recurrent increases in suspected AWD cases in multiple governorates, including Amran, Al Hudaydah, and Hajjah. These align with observed linelist trends and reinforce the importance of multisectoral coordination—particularly WASH and case management integration—to prevent secondary transmission.

Conclusions

  • Sustained community transmission with recurrent spikes during the rainy season.
  • High proportion of paediatric cases, suggesting gaps in household water treatment and hygiene practices.
  • Effective clinical management where CTUs are functional, but uneven geographical access to care.
  • Need for continuous monitoring of case-fatality rates, dehydration severity, and confirmation status.
  • Stronger linkage between surveillance and WASH interventions is essential to sustain control and anticipate new surges.

This interpretation is based on simulated data and public information only. It serves as an operational prototype illustrating how field teams could integrate routine linelist and external signal data for rapid situational awareness.
